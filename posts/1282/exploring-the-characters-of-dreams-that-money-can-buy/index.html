II. The Significance of Their Relationship in the Film#

In Dreams That Money Can Buy, a 1947 surrealist film by German-American director Hans Richter, the audience is introduced to Joe, a struggling artist. Joe, desperate for money to support himself and his art, makes a rather Faustian bargain with a mysterious benefactor, Mr. Sladen. Sladen provides Joe with a briefcase full of cash to fund his projects, but in exchange, Joe must sell him seven of his dreams. As Joe embarks on this strange journey, a complex relationship begins to form between him and his benefactor.

Throughout the film, Joe attempts to stumble his way through a series of haphazardly crafted dreamscapes in order to procure the desired dreams, while at the same time trying to find a way out of this Faustian bargain with Mr. Sladen. The audience also sees the confusing and often tumultuous relationship between Joe and Mr. Sladen - a relationship that is both underpinned by a powerful longing for freedom and an inescapable sense of mutual dependence.

This strange bond between Joe and Mr. Sladen is of great importance to the film as it adds depth and complexity to the many surrealist themes explored throughout. The dreamscapes Joe creates with his imagination serve both as a reflection of his creativity and as a means of escaping the power of Mr. Sladen. As this intricate relationship unfolds, the audience gets to witness the power of dreams and how they can be used to create, manipulate, and ultimately escape from reality.

The relationship between Joe and Mr. Sladen also serves to emphasize the importance of imagination in creating our own reality, as well as the danger of getting in too deep when making a Faustian bargain. Ultimately, Dreams That Money Can Buy provides an intimate look into the complex and interwoven relationship between artist, benefactor, and dreamscapes.
